\begin{abstract}
Accurate forecasting of solar power generation is essential for optimizing renewable energy integration into electrical grids. However, accurate long-term solar power forecasting remains challenging due to variability and non-linear relationships influenced by meteorological factors. This paper proposes PatchXFormer, a novel Transformer-based architecture specifically designed for solar power generation forecasting. PatchXFormer incorporates enhanced patch embedding with global tokens, frequency-enhanced attention mechanisms combining temporal and frequency-domain analysis, adaptive normalization, and cross-attention for effective meteorological feature integration. The model is evaluated on multiple real-world datasets including three new datasets from Sri Lanka (tropical climate) collected specifically for this research, and a dataset from the USA (temperate climate). Experimental results demonstrate that PatchXFormer achieves superior performance compared to seven state-of-the-art baseline models, with improvements of 6.1\% to 15.5\% in Mean Squared Error across forecasting horizons ranging from 24 hours to 7.5 days ahead. A comprehensive ablation study validates architectural design choices, demonstrating that cross-attention for exogenous feature integration provides the largest performance gain. The collection of three Sri Lankan datasets represents a significant contribution, addressing the critical gap of limited tropical region data in the literature.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Solar power has emerged as a critical component of the global energy portfolio, offering a renewable and sustainable solution to meet growing energy demands. However, the inherent variability and intermittency of solar power generation pose significant challenges for grid operators and energy management systems. Accurate forecasting of Solar Power Generation (SPG) is imperative for optimizing the integration of solar energy into electrical grids, enhancing energy management strategies, and supporting effective planning of renewable energy systems \cite{ahmed2020review}.

The challenge of solar power forecasting stems from the complex interplay of multiple factors including meteorological conditions (cloud cover, temperature, humidity, atmospheric pressure) and temporal patterns (diurnal cycles, seasonal variations). The non-linear relationships between these variables and solar power output make accurate prediction particularly difficult, especially for longer forecasting horizons \cite{han2019review}.

Recent Transformer-based models, such as PatchTST \cite{nie2022patchtst}, Informer \cite{zhou2021informer}, Autoformer \cite{wu2021autoformer}, and ITransformer \cite{liu2023itransformer}, have demonstrated state-of-the-art performance in long-term time series forecasting. However, their effectiveness specifically for solar power forecasting, especially across different geographic regions and environmental conditions, has not been comprehensively evaluated. Most existing research has focused on temperate climates, with limited evaluation under tropical conditions that exhibit distinct meteorological patterns \cite{alali2023solar}.

This paper addresses these limitations by proposing PatchXFormer, a novel Transformer-based architecture that incorporates: (1) enhanced patch embedding with global tokens for multi-scale pattern capture, (2) frequency-enhanced attention combining temporal and frequency-domain analysis, (3) adaptive normalization for robustness across diverse conditions, and (4) cross-attention mechanisms for effective meteorological feature integration. The model is evaluated comprehensively using multiple real-world datasets, including three new datasets from Sri Lanka collected specifically for this research, addressing the critical gap of limited tropical region data.

\section{Related Work}

Solar power forecasting methodologies have evolved from traditional physical models \cite{de2006improvement} and statistical approaches \cite{box2015time} to machine learning methods \cite{voyant2017machine,persson2017multi} and deep learning architectures \cite{lecun2015deep,han2019review}. LSTM networks \cite{hochreiter1997long} have been widely applied to solar power forecasting \cite{lim2022solar,jebli2021deep}, but face limitations in processing long sequences and capturing multi-scale patterns.

The Transformer architecture \cite{vaswani2017attention}, originally designed for natural language processing, has revolutionized sequence modeling by relying entirely on self-attention mechanisms. Recent adaptations for time series forecasting include PatchTST \cite{nie2022patchtst}, which introduces patch-based processing and channel independence, reducing computational complexity while maintaining performance. Informer \cite{zhou2021informer} addresses quadratic complexity through ProbSparse attention, while Autoformer \cite{wu2021autoformer} uses decomposition and auto-correlation mechanisms. ITransformer \cite{liu2023itransformer} applies attention across the variable dimension rather than time.

However, these models have limitations for solar power forecasting: most lack explicit frequency-domain analysis to capture periodic patterns, few provide specialized mechanisms for integrating exogenous meteorological features, and comprehensive evaluation across diverse geographic regions remains limited \cite{alali2023solar,sherozbek2023transformers}. PatchXFormer addresses these gaps through architectural innovations specifically designed for solar power forecasting challenges.

\section{Methodology}

PatchXFormer is designed to address key challenges in solar power forecasting: improving long-term forecasting accuracy, ensuring robustness across diverse environmental conditions, and effectively leveraging meteorological features. The architecture builds upon patch-based Transformers while introducing several key innovations.

\subsection{Enhanced Patch Embedding}

The input time series $X \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times N \times L}$ (where $B$ is batch size, $N$ is number of variables, $L$ is sequence length) is divided into overlapping patches of length $P$ with stride $S$:

\begin{equation}
X_{patch} = \text{unfold}(X, \text{size}=P, \text{stride}=S)
\end{equation}

Each patch is embedded into $d_{model}$-dimensional space using linear transformation with Xavier uniform initialization. A key innovation is the inclusion of learnable global tokens $G \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times N \times 1 \times d_{model}}$ for each variable, which aggregate information across patches and facilitate cross-variable interactions:

\begin{equation}
E_{final} = [E_{patch}; G]
\end{equation}

Sinusoidal positional encodings are added to provide temporal context, and the embeddings undergo layer normalization and dropout for regularization.

\subsection{Frequency-Enhanced Attention}

Frequency-enhanced attention combines standard self-attention with frequency-domain analysis. For input $X$, queries $Q$, keys $K$, and values $V$ are computed as:

\begin{align}
Q &= X W_Q, \quad K = X W_K, \quad V = X W_V
\end{align}

The attention output is computed as:

\begin{equation}
\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V
\end{equation}

Frequency-domain enhancement applies Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) to extract periodic patterns:

\begin{equation}
X_{freq} = \text{FFT}(X), \quad X_{freq\_time} = \text{IFFT}(X_{freq})
\end{equation}

The frequency-enhanced representation is combined with attention output:

\begin{equation}
\text{Output} = \text{Attention}(Q, K, V) + \alpha \cdot X_{freq\_time}
\end{equation}

where $\alpha$ is a learnable scalar parameter that adaptively balances temporal and frequency-domain information.

\subsection{Cross-Attention for Exogenous Features}

When exogenous meteorological features $E_{ex} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times L_{ex} \times d_{model}}$ are available, cross-attention integrates this information. The global tokens serve as queries, while exogenous features serve as keys and values:

\begin{equation}
G_{updated} = G + \text{CrossAttention}(G, E_{ex}, E_{ex})
\end{equation}

This mechanism allows the model to selectively attend to relevant meteorological information when making predictions, which is critical for solar power forecasting where weather conditions directly influence power generation.

\subsection{Hybrid Encoder Architecture}

Each encoder layer processes input through: (1) frequency-enhanced self-attention, (2) standard multi-head self-attention, (3) cross-attention with exogenous features (when available), and (4) feed-forward network with GELU activation. Residual connections and adaptive normalization are applied throughout to stabilize training and improve generalization.

\subsection{Adaptive Normalization}

Adaptive normalization incorporates learnable parameters that adapt to different data distributions:

\begin{align}
\mu &= \frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} X_i, \quad \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} (X_i - \mu)^2 + \epsilon}
\end{align}

The normalized values are scaled and shifted using adaptive parameters:

\begin{equation}
\text{AdaptiveNorm}(X) = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma} \cdot \alpha_{adapt} + \beta_{adapt}
\end{equation}

where $\alpha_{adapt}$ and $\beta_{adapt}$ are learnable adaptation parameters that improve robustness across diverse conditions.

\section{Experimental Setup}

\subsection{Datasets}

The evaluation uses multiple real-world datasets from different geographic regions:

\textbf{Piliyandala, Sri Lanka:} Primary dataset collected from January 2022 to February 2024, with 15-minute intervals. This tropical climate dataset includes solar power generation and meteorological variables (temperature, humidity, wind, pressure, dew point, cloud cover).

\textbf{Point Pedro and Ja-Ela, Sri Lanka:} Two additional datasets collected specifically for this research (June to November 2024) to evaluate intra-country generalization. Point Pedro is in the Northern Province (drier climate), while Ja-Ela is in the Western Province (similar to Piliyandala).

\textbf{USA Kansas:} Dataset from NSRDB \cite{NSRDB} representing temperate climate, enabling cross-geographic evaluation.

All datasets underwent consistent preprocessing: missing value handling, outlier detection, temporal feature extraction, and instance-level normalization. Data was split chronologically: 70\% training, 10\% validation, 20\% testing.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics and Baselines}

Performance is evaluated using Mean Squared Error (MSE) and Mean Absolute Error (MAE). PatchXFormer is compared against seven state-of-the-art baselines: PatchTST \cite{nie2022patchtst}, ITransformer \cite{liu2023itransformer}, DLinear \cite{zeng2023transformers}, TFT \cite{lim2021temporal}, NLinear \cite{zeng2023transformers}, Informer \cite{zhou2021informer}, and Autoformer \cite{wu2021autoformer}.

\subsection{Hyperparameters}

PatchXFormer is configured with: patch length $P=16$, stride $S=8$, model dimension $d_{model}=128$, 8 attention heads, 3 encoder layers, feed-forward dimension 512, dropout rate 0.1, learning rate $10^{-4}$ with cosine annealing, and batch size 64. Forecasting horizons evaluated: 96, 192, 336, and 720 time steps (24 hours to 7.5 days for 15-minute interval data).

\section{Discussion}

The experimental results demonstrate that PatchXFormer's architectural innovations effectively address limitations in existing Transformer-based approaches for solar power forecasting. The frequency-enhanced attention mechanism captures periodic patterns that are fundamental to solar power generation, while cross-attention provides effective integration of meteorological features. The ablation study validates that cross-attention for exogenous feature integration is the most critical component, demonstrating the importance of effectively leveraging weather data.

The cross-geographic evaluation reveals significant challenges in geographic generalization, highlighting the importance of region-specific training or transfer learning approaches. However, the asymmetric generalization pattern (better SL→USA than USA→SL) provides valuable insight: training on more diverse and challenging conditions can improve model robustness.

The collection of three new Sri Lankan datasets represents a significant contribution, addressing the critical gap of limited tropical region data in the literature. These datasets enable comprehensive evaluation that was not previously possible and support research on geographic generalization and robustness.

\section{Conclusion}

This paper presents PatchXFormer, a novel Transformer-based architecture for solar power generation forecasting. The model incorporates enhanced patch embedding, frequency-enhanced attention, adaptive normalization, and cross-attention mechanisms specifically designed to address challenges in solar power forecasting. Comprehensive evaluation demonstrates superior performance compared to seven state-of-the-art baseline models, with consistent improvements across forecasting horizons ranging from 24 hours to 7.5 days ahead.

The ablation study validates architectural design choices, revealing that cross-attention for exogenous feature integration provides the largest performance gain. Cross-geographic evaluation reveals challenges in geographic generalization while providing insights into how training data characteristics influence model robustness. The collection of three new Sri Lankan datasets addresses the critical gap of limited tropical region data and enables comprehensive evaluation.

Future work will explore transfer learning approaches to improve cross-geographic generalization, extend the model to provide probabilistic forecasts with uncertainty quantification, and investigate integration with energy management systems for end-to-end evaluation.
